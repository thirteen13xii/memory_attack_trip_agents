### Section 1: Daily Itineraries & Enrichment

#### Day 1: Tokyo - Arrival and Ascent

**Morning & Afternoon:** You'll touch down at Narita (NRT) or Haneda (HND). From there, the most efficient way to the city center is the **Narita Express (N'EX)** or **Limousine Bus**. After checking into the sublime **Aman Tokyo**, take a moment to breathe. The lobby alone, with its 30-meter high ceiling and views of the Imperial Palace, is a destination in itself.

- **2:00 PM | Tokyo Skytree:** Excellent choice for your first introduction to Tokyo's scale. **Insider Tip:** Book a ticket for the *Tembo Galleria* (the higher observation deck) online in advance. It offers a stunning 360-degree view and a glass-floored walkway. The view at dusk, as the city lights begin to twinkle, is nothing short of magical.
- **7:00 PM | Dinner at Sukiyabashi Jiro:** This is a pilgrimage for sushi lovers. **Critical Advice:**
    - **Booking:** This is incredibly difficult. Your hotel concierge is your best bet. Secure a recommendation *months* in advance.
    - **Etiquette:** Be prompt, do not wear strong perfume/cologne (it interferes with the fragrance of the fish), and eat the sushi as soon as it's placed before you.
    - **Mindset:** It's an efficient, focused experience. Savor each piece and the master's artistry.

**My Personal Recommendation:** Before dinner, take a stroll through the **Ginza** district. It’s the epicenter of high-end shopping and is beautifully illuminated at night.

#### Day 2: Tokyo - Sumo Spirit & Culinary Art

**10:00 AM | Grand Sumo Tournament:** If it's taking place (tournaments are held in January, May, and September), this is an electrifying cultural experience. **Insider Tip:**
    - **Tickets:** Sell out instantly. Book online as soon as they are released, or ask your hotel to purchase through a Japanese agency.
    - **Experience:** The lower, ringside seats (masu-seki) are a box, so be prepared to sit on a cushion. The upper-level seats are standard chairs. Arrive early (morning) to watch lower-ranked wrestlers; the top-division matches don't start until the afternoon.
    - **Food:** Try a *chanko nabe* bento box, the hearty stew sumo wrestlers eat to gain weight!

**1:00 PM | Lunch at Afuri:** A perfect, light lunch after the intensity of the sumo. The **yuzu shio ramen** is a masterpiece of clean, citrusy flavor. It’s a counter-service operation, so expect a short line, but it moves fast.

**7:00 PM | Dinner at Narisawa:** This is a world-class dining experience. **Advice:**
    - **Wine Pairing:** I highly recommend the wine or sake pairing. It’s expertly curated to complement each of the 10+ courses.
    - **The Dish:** The "Bread of the Forest 2010" is legendary, baked tableside in a mini-terrarium using natural yeast from the forest. Don't leave without trying it.
    - **Attire:** Smart casual is fine, but avoid shorts and flip-flops.

#### Day 3: The Shinkansen to Timeless Kyoto

**9:00 AM | The Journey:** This is an experience in itself. Board the **Shinkansen (bullet train)** from Tokyo Station. Book a seat on the **right-hand side** (Hiroshima-bound direction) for a breathtaking view of **Mount Fuji** on a clear day. Grab a *ekiben* (train station bento box) and a beer to make the ~2.5-hour journey a highlight.

**12:00 PM | Hoshinoya Kyoto:** You are entering a world of tranquility. The boat transfer from Togetsukyo Bridge is the only way to access this hotel. It sets the tone perfectly. Upon arrival, participate in the traditional tea ceremony welcome.

**2:00 PM | Fushimi Inari Shrine:** Do not just see the first thousand gates. **Insider Tip:** Climb the mountain trail. It takes about 2 hours to reach the summit. The higher you go, the fewer the crowds and the more serene the experience. The small shrines and cemeteries tucked along the path are fascinating. Wear comfortable shoes!

**7:00 PM | Dinner at Kikunoi:** A pinnacle of *kaiseki* cuisine. **To enhance your evening:**
    - **Sake Pairing:** Let the chef choose your sake. It’s an education in terroir.
    - **Etiquette:** Dress smartly. Appreciate the presentation of each dish as a work of art.

#### Day 4: Kyoto - Festival of Life

**10:00 AM | Gion Matsuri:** If you are here during July, you have hit the jackpot. **Key events:**
    - **Yoiyama (Evenings before the parade):** The streets of Gion become a pedestrian-only festival of food, drink, and traditional music. The floats (yamaboko) are on display.
    - **The Grand Procession (July 17th):** The main parade. Staking a spot along the route early is crucial. The energy is incredible.
    - **Sakura Season:** If in April, replace the festival with **Philosopher’s Path** in the morning. It is a breathtaking 2km cherry blossom tunnel.

**My Personal Recommendation for the Afternoon:** After the festival, escape the crowds. Walk through the **Bamboo Grove** in Arashiyama *very early* (like 6 AM) or just before sunset to avoid the masses. Then, visit the beautiful **Tenryuji Temple** right next door.

**7:00 PM | Dinner at a Local Kyoto Restaurant:** Your schedule leaves this open, which is fantastic. **I recommend:**
    - **Omen Kodai-ji** for amazing, thick *udon* noodles in a modern setting.
    - **Monk Chagama** for a unique, wood-fired pizza and fantastic natural wine in a restored townhouse.
    - **Pontocho Alley:** Just wander. The narrow alley is packed with tiny, atmospheric restaurants. You can often find *kushikatsu* (skewers) or *yakiniku* (grilled meat).

#### Day 5: Hiroshima - A City of Peace & Okonomiyaki

**9:00 AM | The Journey:** Another beautiful Shinkansen ride (approx. 2 hours). Grab a window seat.

**12:00 PM | Peace Memorial Park & Museum:** This is a profoundly moving and essential experience. **How to approach:**
    - **Start at the Museum:** It provides the necessary historical context. It is sobering but incredibly important.
    - **The Park:** Walk to the **A-Bomb Dome**, a UNESCO World Heritage site, the **Children's Peace Monument** (look for the thousands of paper cranes), and the **Flame of Peace**.
    - **Mindset:** Allow yourself time and space to reflect. It's a heavy but crucial part of Japan's complex history.

**7:00 PM | Dinner at Okonomimura:** This is a perfect, hands-on dinner. **Adventure Tip:** Each floor has several tiny stalls. My favorite is on the 2nd floor—look for the one with the longest line of locals. Try the **"Modern Yaki"** , which includes noodles, and add cheese and mochi (rice cake) for the full experience.

**My Personal Recommendation:** If you have time, take a quick ferry from Hiroshima Peace Park to **Miyajima Island** to see the famous "floating" **Itsukushima Shrine** and its giant *torii* gate. It's a serene 2-hour detour.

#### Day 6: Osaka - A Blast of Energy

**9:00 AM | The Journey:** A short, 1.5-hour Shinkansen or express train ride from Hiroshima to Osaka.

**12:00 PM | Osaka Castle:** It's a reconstruction, but the museum inside is excellent and offers a great view. **Better Idea:** Instead of the castle itself, stroll through the sprawling **Osaka Castle Park**. It’s a beautiful green space, especially nice if you have a coffee or *taiyaki* (fish-shaped cake with red bean paste) from a nearby vendor.

**7:00 PM | Dotonbori Konamon Museum:** This is pure, wonderful, loud Osaka. **The Experience:**
    - **Service:** You get a hot griddle and bowls of batter. Don't worry, the staff is incredibly helpful and will guide you through making perfect *okonomiyaki* and *takoyaki*.
    - **Before/After:** This is in the heart of **Dotonbori**, the city's neon-lit entertainment district. Walk the canal, marvel at the giant mechanical signs (the Glico Running Man, the giant crab at Kani Doraku), and soak in the electric atmosphere.

#### Day 7: Departure

**10:00 AM | Departure from Osaka (KIX):** Your trip concludes. Use the efficient **Nankai Line** or a limousine bus. **Last Tip:** If you have time, grab some last-minute *omiyage* (souvenirs) at the airport, like Kit Kat bars in matcha or sake flavors.

### Section 2: Dining Suggestions & Culinary Deep Dives

Beyond your scheduled meals, let’s talk about the *other* essential foods of Japan.

- **Breakfast Options:**
    - **Japanese Breakfast:** Your hotel (especially Aman and Hoshinoya) will offer a spectacular, multi-dish traditional breakfast. Do not skip it! It includes grilled fish, rice, miso soup, pickles, and *natto* (fermented soybeans).
    - **Coffee & Toast:** For a quicker start, find a chain like **Komeda's Coffee** or **Doutor** for a classic Japanese morning set: toast, a boiled egg, and coffee.
- **Street Food & Snacks (Must-Try):**
    - **Taiyaki:** Fish-shaped cake with red bean paste or custard. *(Kyoto/Osaka)*
    - **Takoyaki:** Octopus balls, crispy on the outside, molten on the inside. *(Osaka is the best)*
    - **Kakigori:** Shaved ice topped with flavored syrup, condensed milk, and fruit. *(Perfect for summer)*
    - **Ramen:** Your Afuri lunch is great. Also try **Ichiran** (solo booths, very focused) or a local *tonkotsu* (pork bone broth) shop.
- **Convenience Store Wisdom:** The most important tip: **7-Eleven, Lawson, and Family Mart** are your best friends. They have incredible onigiri (rice balls), egg salad sandwiches, fried chicken (Karaage-kun), and cheap beer. They are a lifesaver.

### Section 3: Practical Tips for the Savvy Traveler

1.  **Getting Around:**
    - **SUICA/PASMO Card:** The ultimate key. An IC card works on subways, trains, and buses in all your cities, and you can use it at convenience stores. Buy one at any train station and load it with cash.
    - **JR Pass?** You are traveling Tokyo-Kyoto-Hiroshima-Osaka. A **7-day JR Pass** will likely pay for itself. Check a JR Pass calculator online with your exact dates to be sure.

2.  **Money & Payments:**
    - **Cash is King.** Unlike the West, many smaller restaurants, temples, and local shops in Kyoto/Osaka are cash-only. Always have 20,000-30,000 yen on hand.
    - **Credit Cards:** Accepted at major hotels, department stores, and nice restaurants, but not everywhere.

3.  **Internet & Connectivity:**
    - **Pocket WiFi:** Rent one from a company like **Japan Wireless** or **Sakura Mobile**. Pick it up at the airport. Essential for maps, translation, and social media.
    - **eSIM:** If your phone is unlocked, an eSIM (like Ubigi or Airalo) is even easier.

4.  **Etiquette & Culture:**
    - **Bowing:** A slight bow of the head is a standard, polite greeting.
    - **Shoes:** Take your shoes off when entering a home, *ryokan* (Hoshinoya), and many traditional restaurants. Indoor slippers are provided. Never wear slippers into a room with tatami mats.
    - **Onsen (Hot Springs):** Both the Aman and Hoshinoya will likely have a bath. *No tattoos* is a strict rule in most traditional onsens. If you have small ones, you can cover them with a bandage. At these luxury hotels, it's becoming more relaxed, but always check.
    - **Silence on Trains:** It is considered very rude to talk on the phone in commuter trains. Announcements are made. Be mindful of the quiet atmosphere.

5.  **Stay Charged:**
    - **Power Plugs:** The same as the US (two flat prongs, Type A). Voltage is 100V.

6.  **Phrase Check:**
    - **Sumimasen (soo-mee-mah-sen):** The magic word. It means "excuse me," "sorry," and "thank you" (for a service). Use it constantly.
    - **Arigato gozaimasu (ah-ree-gah-toh goh-zai-mahs):** "Thank you very much." Polished, formal, and appreciated.
    - **Konnichiwa:** "Hello/Good afternoon."

7.  **Pacing is Key:** You have a packed but fantastic schedule. The biggest mistake travelers make is trying to do too much. You have built in wonderful, immersive experiences. **Take your time.** Savor a second glass of sake. Spend an extra 15 minutes sitting in a quiet temple garden. The best memories are often the unplanned ones.

You have an incredible adventure ahead of you. Japan is a place that respects rules but rewards the curious, the polite, and the open-hearted. Go with an empty soul, and you will return full.
